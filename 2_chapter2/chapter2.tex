This chapter reviews literature on ageing, chronic disease, and predictive modelling using \gls{TILDA} (introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter1}). It covers four interrelated domains: physical function and \gls{FR}, \gls{MH}, nutrition and biological ageing, and \gls{ML} applications for chronic disease prediction.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Search strategy and study selection}
This review applied systematic mapping principles~\cite{petersenGuidelinesConductingSystematic2015}: research questions were defined, relevant databases searched, inclusion and exclusion criteria applied, and findings synthesised through an integrative approach~\cite{torracoWritingIntegrativeLiterature2005,kitchenhamSystematicLiteratureReviews2009a}. The primary focus was chronic disease prediction in older adults using \gls{TILDA}, where \gls{ML} applications within \gls{TILDA} were limited, the scope was extended to include comparable ageing cohort studies.

\subsection{Database search and query development}
The main topic of interest was chronic disease prediction using nutrition, lifestyle, and clinical data from \gls{TILDA}. Relevant databases (e.g., ScienceDirect, PubMed) were searched using combinations of keywords such as ``Irish Longitudinal Study on Ageing'', ``machine learning'', ``chronic disease'', ``nutrition'', ``diet'', and ``physical activity'', as shown in Table~\ref{tab:PetersenSearch}. 

The search covered studies published from 2011 to 2025. Figure~\ref{fig:mapping_years} summarises the number of studies identified per year, highlighting trends and gaps over time.

\subsection{Quality and relevance evaluation} 
To systematically assess the quality of the included studies, a customised version of the \gls{NOS} was applied (Table~\ref{tab:NOS}). 
The \gls{NOS} evaluates studies across three domains: \emph{selection} (S1--S4), \emph{comparability} (C1--C2), and \emph{outcome} (O1--O3). 
Selection criteria assess the representativeness of the sample, clarity of exposure measurement, and temporal alignment of outcomes. 
Comparability examines the control of key confounders (variables that may influence both the predictor and outcome), while outcome evaluates measurement validity, follow up adequacy, and completeness. 
Each domain is scored binary (0 = criterion not met, 1 = criterion met), and the total reflects overall study quality.

This initial table guided a further refinement of the results into two sub tables: 1.studies critically related to \gls{TILDA}, 2.studies using \gls{TILDA} and \gls{ML} for predictive contexts, which revealed minimal to no integration with \gls{TILDA}.

\section{Physical function and frailty}
\gls{FR} reflects declining physiological reserve, providing a framework for understanding physical vulnerability in ageing. 
In \gls{TILDA}, it is captured through both the \gls{FR} phenotype and the \gls{FR} index, with \gls{UGS}, \gls{HGS}, and mobility decline serving as practical predictors. 
These measures strongly predict hospitalisation and mortality.

\subsection{Probabilistic interpretation of frailty}
From a probabilistic perspective, the likelihood of an adverse outcome $Y$ (such as mortality) given observed \gls{FR} features $X$ is expressed as a conditional probability $P(Y \mid X)$, and identifying which features carry the strongest predictive signal requires understanding how much unique information each contributes. 

When features $X_i$ and $X_j$ are statistically independent,
\begin{equation*}
P(X_i, X_j) = P(X_i)\,P(X_j),
\end{equation*}
and each adds fully independent information to the model. In practice, however, \gls{FR} related predictors such as gait speed, grip strength, and mobility limitation are often highly correlated, so the joint distribution does not factorise and the marginal gain from adding a second correlated predictor is reduced. This has direct implications for feature selection in \gls{ML}, as discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5}.

Evidence suggests that \gls{FR} is dynamic, not irreversible. 
For example, multi state modelling showed that many prefrail adults transition back to non frail, although established \gls{FR} strongly predicts mortality~\cite{romero-ortunoTransitionsFrailtyPhenotype2021}. 
Metabolic syndrome accelerates the development of \gls{FR}, suggesting a metabolic pathway of vulnerability~\cite{mccarthyAssociationMetabolicSyndrome2023}. 
The presence of metabolic syndrome modifies the conditional probability $P(\text{\gls{FR}} \mid \text{Metabolic Syndrome})$ and may increase the likelihood of adverse outcomes over time. 
Age also plays an important role: obesity predicts mobility decline earlier in life,  whereas later in life, traits that protect against this, including \gls{HGS}, become more significant~\cite{huangAgeModifyAssociations2019}. 

These findings partly conflict: \gls{FR} appears both reversible and progressive, depending on risk context. This complexity motivates probabilistic models that can update beliefs about risk as new observations arrive, as formalised by Bayes theorem:
\begin{equation*}
P(Y \mid X) = \frac{P(X \mid Y) P(Y)}{P(X)}
\end{equation*}
where $P(Y \mid X)$ is the posterior probability of outcome $Y$ given features $X$. In practice, causal inference remains limited by observational designs and predictors are frequently examined in isolation. Although formal Bayesian inference is not implemented in this study, this probabilistic framing motivates the use of well calibrated \gls{ML} models evaluated via \gls{AUC} and the Brier score, as described in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5}.

\section{Mental health in older adults}\label{sc:mental_health}
\gls{MH}, including depression, anxiety, loneliness, and \gls{SOC} isolation, strongly shapes quality of life. 
Evidence from \gls{TILDA} shows strong links to both physical and \gls{SOC} factors.~\cite{santiniProtectivePropertiesActBelongCommit2017} found that participation in physical, \gls{SOC}, and cognitive activities summarised by the Act-Belong-Commit (ABC) indicator was associated with lower depression and anxiety. 
This suggests \gls{SOC} integration and lifestyle protect mental wellbeing. However,~\cite{juolaO117NursingStaff2014} reported that self reported measures may exaggerate such associations, raising concerns of bias. 
These two findings conflict: the effect of \gls{SOC} engagement is strong when self reported and weaker when objectively assessed.

Objective data strengthen the case for activity. Accelerometer based measures show a dose response link between moderate to vigorous activity and lower depressive symptoms~\cite{lairdPhysicalActivityDose2023}. 
Yet reverse pathways are also evident: incident falls and functional decline predict later depression, mediated by \gls{FOF}~\cite{jacobFallsIncreaseRisk2023}. 
Together, these results reveal a bidirectional relationship, \gls{MH} both influences and is influenced by physical decline. Most studies rely on broad self report scales, such as \gls{CESD} or \gls{HADS}, with little biomarker or clinical validation. 

This literature suggests \gls{MH} in ageing requires longitudinal, multidimensional approaches capable of capturing bidirectional pathways. 
Formally, the conditional probability
\begin{equation*}
P(\text{Depression} \mid \text{Activity, Falls, \gls{SOC} Engagement})
\end{equation*}
quantifies how these interacting features jointly influence \gls{MH} outcomes, indicating which predictors carry the strongest signal and which may be redundant.

\section{Nutrition and biological ageing}
Nutrition interacts with both biological and psychosocial systems, shaping ageing trajectories and \gls{FR}. 
Biomarkers like vitamin D, lutein, and zeaxanthin have been linked to physical and cognitive outcomes in \gls{TILDA}.~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023} showed that higher plasma lutein and zeaxanthin dietary carotenoids found in leafy vegetables were linked to slower \gls{FR} progression. 
By contrast,~\cite{mccarthyAssociationVitaminDeficiency2022} found vitamin D deficiency predicted \gls{DM}, musculoskeletal decline, especially among adults with low sunlight exposure. 
These studies point to different nutritional mechanisms: antioxidant protection versus metabolic regulation. Identifying which biomarkers are most informative for feature selection in \gls{ML} pipelines remains an open question.

\subsection{Contextual and integrative considerations}
Malnutrition also reflects \gls{SOC} and functional risks.~\cite{bardonPredictorsIncidentMalnutrition2020} found that hospitalisation, functional loss, and poor \gls{SOC} support increased malnutrition risk, with notable sex differences. 
This highlights a limitation in much nutritional research: biological markers are studied without integrating \gls{SOC} context.

The difference between biological and chronological age is a major point of debate. 
Biomarker based metrics that better capture these cumulative exposures, like \gls{FR} indices or epigenetic clocks, frequently vary from chronological age. 
McCarthy and Azizi link metabolic syndrome and nutritional deficits to accelerated biological ageing~\cite{mccarthyMetabolicSyndromeAccelerates2023,aziziEvidenceAssociationAllostatic2024}. 
Although the majority of the evidence is cross sectional and has little ability to capture changes over time, these indicators offer a more precise prediction of vulnerability. 
Reliability is also reduced in biomarker studies with small sample sizes, limiting confidence in the estimated effect magnitudes.

Taken together, nutrition interacts with biological, metabolic, and \gls{SOC} systems. 
Isolating single nutrients risks oversimplification. Integrative modelling that combines biomarkers, clinical factors, and \gls{SOC} determinants supported by machine learning pipelines offers a more promising route. Models that estimate the joint distribution of features and outcomes can better capture the complex dependencies relevant to biological ageing.

\section{Machine Learning in ageing and chronic disease}
\gls{ML} is increasingly applied in ageing research because it can capture nonlinear and multidimensional relationships that traditional regression models, such as \gls{LR}, often miss~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023, boyleBrainpredictedAgeDifference2021}. Studies using \gls{ML} in ageing research include many types of predictors: clinical measures, sociodemographic factors, lifestyle habits, cognitive tests, and psychosocial variables. 

A recurring pattern is that models rely primarily on readily measured clinical data $-$ comorbidity counts, mobility scores, and vital signs, while nutrition, \gls{SOC}, and lifestyle predictors remain underrepresented~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023,moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021}.
This imbalance may limit the ability of models to capture the full complexity of chronic disease risk with ageing, especially considering that \gls{MH} and nutrition exert independent and interacting effects on later life outcomes. 
Models that rely solely on readily available clinical data risk reproducing the same biases seen in traditional regression approaches.

% \subsection{Models used in machine learning}
\subsection{Methodological approaches}
The methodological approaches used in ageing research span both traditional and advanced models. 
\gls{LR} and Markov models remain common baselines due to their interpretability and simplicity, yet they are often unable to capture nonlinearities or higher order interactions. 
More recent work has shifted towards ensemble methods such as \gls{RF}, \gls{GBM}, as well as \gls{DNN}s, which generally achieve higher predictive accuracy.

For instance, ~\cite{matthewsPredictingMortalityIrish2022} evaluated mortality prediction using \gls{LR} with demographic, clinical, and functional predictors, achieving an \gls{AUC} of $0.78$. In contrast, ~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023} reported an \gls{AUC} of $0.854$ using a stacked ensemble for \gls{DM} prediction, illustrating that flexible nonlinear models can better capture complex interactions among ageing related predictors.

\subsection{Performance and validation}
Predictive performance across studies is generally moderate to strong, with \gls{AUC} values between 0.70 and 0.90 for outcomes like \gls{DM}, \gls{UGS}, depression, and mortality. 
This indicates that models can distinguish reasonably well between individuals who will or will not experience an outcome. 

However, there are important limitations. Many studies rely only on internal validation, testing models on the same data used for training. 
This can inflate performance estimates and reduce confidence in applying the model to new populations. 
Furthermore, most studies are cross sectional, so causal or temporal effects cannot be assessed. 
When combined with the underuse of nutrition and \gls{SOC} predictors, these issues reduce the generalisability of existing models~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023}. % ~\cite{zarudskyP277OneleggedBalance2014}

\subsection{Explainability in machine learning for ageing}
As \gls{ML} models become more complex, interpretability emerges as a critical challenge. Explainability tools like \gls{SHAP} can highlight feature importance, but their use is inconsistent across studies, particularly in deep learning models~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023,davisLinearRegressionbasedMachine2021,davisComparisonGaitSpeed2021}. 
These methods improve transparency by revealing which features drive predictions, yet black-box concerns persist, particularly in deep learning, where feature interactions are difficult to track.
For ageing research, where clinical adoption requires both accuracy and trust, explainability is not optional but essential. 
Current efforts show promise but remain fragmented, with no consensus on best practice. 
This gap links directly to the broader methodological limits discussed in the next section on gaps in the literature.

\section{Gaps in the literature}\label{sc:gaps_literature}
First, most studies rely on \gls{TILDA} or similar cohorts of Irish and predominantly older adults $\texttt{(50+)}$. This limits confidence in how well results transfer to other populations. This thesis focuses on \gls{TILDA} in depth, using all five available longitudinal waves for model development and evaluation. Cross study replication using the \gls{RAND} Harmonised \gls{TILDA} dataset, which maps variables to international standards compatible with comparable cohorts such as \gls{HRS} and \gls{SHARE}, is identified as a direction for future work.

Second, there is an over reliance on physical health predictors such as comorbidity counts and mobility measures, while nutrition, \gls{SOC}, religious or spiritual, and environmental variables are often overlooked. As shown in~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023}, nutritional biomarkers like lutein and zeaxanthin are strong predictors of \gls{FR}, yet these are rarely integrated into broader \gls{ML} pipelines. By contrast, studies such as~\cite{bardonPredictorsIncidentMalnutrition2020} highlight the role of \gls{SOC} support and functional decline in malnutrition risk. Together, these examples show that excluding nutrition and \gls{SOC} factors risks producing models that reinforce the same biomedical biases seen in regression heavy work. Whether nutritional and psychosocial predictors provide independent signal beyond established health burden indicators in this cohort is empirically tested in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5}.

Third, methodological choices remain conservative. Regression models remain dominant, likely due to their interpretability and ease of implementation. However, they often fail to capture nonlinear interactions and higher order dependencies present in ageing related data. Neural network architectures beyond standard regression are rarely explored in this literature. This study includes a feedforward \gls{MLP} as a baseline neural network family alongside tree ensemble and linear methods. For instance,~\cite{matthewsPredictingMortalityIrish2022} achieved an \gls{AUC} of 0.78 using \gls{LR} for mortality prediction in \gls{TILDA}, whereas~\cite{xuTailoredMachineLearning2023} reported an \gls{AUC} of 0.854 with ensemble methods for \gls{DM} incidence. This contrast illustrates that more flexible \gls{ML} models can provide superior predictive performance while maintaining interpretability when combined with explainability techniques. Where ensembles or \gls{brainPAD} approaches have been attempted, validation is almost always internal, with little cross cohort testing. Weak validation and narrow feature sets limit the generalisability of these models and increase the risk of overfitting.

Fourth, many outcomes relevant to older adults such as pain, sleep quality, and subjective wellbeing remain underexplored. For example,~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023} focused on plasma biomarkers for \gls{FR} without assessing sleep or pain, and \gls{UGS} models such as~\cite{davisComparisonGaitSpeed2021} similarly omit subjective quality of life. Omitting these outcomes limits the real world application of predictive models. Including subjective outcomes such as pain and wellbeing alongside clinical diagnoses would produce more actionable risk profiles for interventions targeting older adults.

Fifth, cross sectional bias and data handling weaken reliability. Much nutritional work, such as~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023}, relies on single time point biomarker data, which cannot capture longitudinal change. By contrast, studies applying multi wave \gls{TILDA} data~\cite{romero-ortunoTransitionsFrailtyPhenotype2021} demonstrate the added value of modelling temporal dynamics. Similarly, missing data are often handled through listwise deletion, as in~\cite{matthewsPredictingMortalityIrish2022}, which risks biasing results toward healthier participants, whereas more robust imputation approaches remain underused.

Finally, interpretability challenges persist, even accurate models may face barriers to clinical acceptance. Taken together, these examples show that ageing research continues to rely on narrow predictors, conservative models, and weak validation. Addressing these gaps requires integrative, diverse, and transparent approaches that link multidimensional predictors with explainable \gls{ML}.

\section{Chapter summary}
This chapter reviewed evidence on ageing and chronic disease across \gls{FR} and physical function, \gls{MH} and nutrition, alongside \gls{ML} applications for predicting later life outcomes.

The findings showed that while \gls{FR} is partly reversible, it remains strongly tied to mortality, that \gls{MH} both shapes and is shaped by physical decline, and that nutrition interacts with biological, metabolic, and \gls{SOC} systems in ways that are often underestimated. 

While machine learning has improved prediction accuracy in ageing research, challenges remain in integrating diverse predictors, ensuring transparent feature attribution, and achieving robust longitudinal validation.

Addressing these challenges requires integrative, longitudinal, and transparent approaches that combine diverse predictors. These gaps motivated the data preparation and pipeline implementation described in Chapters~\ref{ch:chapter3} and~\ref{ch:chapter4}, and the modelling strategies presented in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5}.
