Following manual variable filtering in (Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter3}), the reduced datasets contained only the variables of interest. For example, in Wave 1, the visualiser detected 650 categorical columns, 150 (\texttt{float64}) columns, and 9 object columns, while the harmonised dataset contained 304 categorical columns, 148 \texttt{float32} columns, and several integer columns. Age distributions confirmed that the study population was restricted to older adults across waves, however, ages were bottom coded for participants younger than 50 years and top coded for those aged 65--85 years and above. Missing value summaries indicated substantial variation (e.g., mean missing values ranging from $4.7\%$ in Wave 4 to $35\%$ in the harmonised dataset, with some variables exceeding $77\%$ missing).

Despite this, the exploratory visualiser was limited because the raw data were not yet cleaned, many outputs were incorrect or nonsensical due to hybrid formats and inconsistent coding. For instance, inspection revealed that many variables across both the wave specific and harmonised datasets, while appearing numeric at first glance, in fact contained hybrid representations combining numeric prefixes with descriptive text (e.g., ``1.Rarely or none of the time (less than 1 day)'', ``4.All of the time (5--7 days)'', or prefixed labels such as ``0.not coupled'', ``1.coupled''). A simplistic string stripping approach would therefore produce incorrect numeric values (for example, reducing ``5--7 days'' to \texttt{5}) resulting in silently introducing semantic distortion. Similarly, numeric columns sometimes contained sentinel codes (e.g., -98, -99) or embedded symbols (`$\$$', `$cm$', `$\%$') that skewed visual summaries.

These insights, revealed through systematic profiling, guided the development of the subsequent cleaning pipeline, ensuring consistent numeric logic representations (e.g., midpoints for ranges, standardised sentinel handling).

\section{Cleaning Engine}
The core challenge in converting heterogeneous variable types across the six datasets (Table~\ref{tab:dataset_shape}) was achieving a single numerical standard suitable for downstream analysis. This required enforcing \emph{numerical homogeneity}, defined here as representing all retained features using a single numerical representation (\texttt{float64}) with a unified missing or non response sentinel ($\texttt{-1.0}$). The exploratory profiling across all waves showed that categorical variables were dominant overall ($2,916$ columns), while numeric variables were fragmented across multiple types, with \texttt{float64} being the most prevalent native numeric representation ($462$ columns), motivating its use as a common standard to ensure consistency and compatibility with downstream \gls{ML} models, while accommodating decimal values arising from range midpoints, frequency normalisation, and ordinal scaling that cannot be reliably represented using integer types. To address this, the solution was implemented as a two stage system. The first stage performs automated \emph{variable scenario detection}, identifying the structural form of each variable based on observed value patterns using the \texttt{detect\_column\_scenario\_mapping} function (Appendix~\ref{app:variable_mapping_code}). This stage includes additional diagnostic categories used during development to surface unhandled edge cases.

The second stage applies a deterministic \emph{six step numerical cleaning engine} (Appendix~\ref{app:cleaning_pipeline_code}). While nine scenario categories were initially defined during development, three served exclusively as validation fallbacks and were progressively eliminated as detection coverage improved, with a full scenario to step mapping counts provided in Appendix~\ref{tab:cleaning_tables}. The final pipeline therefore consists of six active transformation steps, applied sequentially and uniformly across all waves.

\subsection{Stage 1: Variable scenario mapping and validation}
Stage 1 focused on automatically identifying how each variable should be handled during cleaning. The automated detection function in Appendix~\ref{app:variable_mapping_code} was developed to classify variables based on their observed value structure, including the presence of sentinel codes, numeric annotations, ordinal labels, and mixed string numeric formats. While multiple intermediate detection categories were used during development, these served solely as internal routing logic. While all variables passed sequentially through the six step cleaning pipeline, most were fully resolved in the earlier steps (Steps 1--5). The final step (Step 6 in Appendix~\ref{app:step6_code}) was applied only to residual variables that could not be deterministically resolved by earlier transformations.

The additional detection categories (e.g., ``single value categorical'', ``special symbol mixed'', and ``unknown/complex'') were intentionally included as diagnostic fallbacks to surface unhandled edge cases due to the complexity and size of the data. As rules matured, variables initially defaulted to these fallback categories were progressively resolved into earlier, well defined scenarios, leaving none remaining for the final pipeline execution.

To ensure robustness, the classification output was immediately processed by a validation routine that generated a comprehensive dictionary of feature states before and after cleaning, including all representative sample values for each variable. This allowed direct inspection of how the same variable was represented across waves prior to cleaning, particularly in cases where identical semantic categories appeared in different orders or formats. By comparing these grouped samples before and after each cleaning step, classification errors and inconsistent mappings were identified and corrected prior to execution of subsequent transformations.

The dictionary generated inside the \texttt{show\_grouped\_vars\_for\_scenarios} function (Appendix~\ref{app:md_script}) was formatted to create an automatic friendly ``.md'' file for rapid, surface level verification, which honed the final mapping dictionary (an example can be found in Appendix~\ref{app:md_audit}) for all features across all waves. This process of using automated detection followed by visual, manual validation was essential for correcting errors in classification logic before the subsequent scenario transformations were executed in the pipeline.

\subsection{Stage 2: Deterministic six step cleaning sequence}
The nine input scenarios were processed sequentially by the \emph{six step cleaning engine}, resolving simple and unambiguous cases first to maximise the proportion of clean numerical features before tackling more complex structures, particularly censored and multi-class scenarios (Figure~\ref{fig:cleaning_flow}). With later steps progressively shifting from cleaning into semantic feature engineering, this deterministic sequence applied identical transformation and feature encoding logic to each wave, with later steps (particularly Steps 4--6) performing rule-based feature engineering by deriving semantically meaningful numerical representations directly from observed category tokens at runtime. The methodological rationale and implications of this integrated engineering which reframes cleaning as semantic feature construction are detailed in Section~\ref{subsec:feature_engineering}. (See Appendix~\ref{app:step6_code} for implementation).

This design reflects the empirical diversity of survey encodings, where a small subset of variables only reveal their semantic structure after simpler transformations have been applied.

The final cleaning pipeline consisted of six deterministic steps applied uniformly across all \gls{TILDA} waves, with increasingly complex transformations handled in later stages. Steps 4--6 required particular attention due to hybrid, ordinal, or multi-class formats. A summary of each step and examples is provided below.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Empty / Blank Values:} All missing entries, empty strings, and whitespace were converted to the standard non-response sentinel ($\texttt{-1.0}$).
    
    \item \textbf{Sentinel Values:} Wave specific non-response codes (e.g., $\texttt{-98}$, $\texttt{-99}$) and textual sentinels (e.g., \texttt{"dk"}, \texttt{"not applicable"}) were mapped to the unified ($\texttt{-1.0}$) placeholder.

    \item \textbf{Numeric Transformation:} Numeric like columns containing embedded units, symbols, or hybrid strings (e.g., $\texttt{50+}$, $\texttt{<10}$, $\texttt{1-2.5}$) were parsed using regex and converted to \texttt{float64}. For ranges, midpoints \&/or boundary values were used. For censored or annotated values, appropriate upper/lower bounds were applied. 

    \item \textbf{Censored / Ordinal Variables:} This step addressed variables representing ordinal scales, bracketed numeric ranges, or frequency expressions (e.g., ``2--3 days a week'', ``daily''). Transformations included:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item[-] Converting textual ordinal labels and Likert scale responses to numeric equivalents.  
        \item[-] Mapping calendar based frequency descriptors to numeric weekly equivalents.  
        \item[-] Manual verification for low count or unusual variables, supported by the Markdown audit outputs (Appendix~\ref{app:md_audit}), to ensure semantic meaning was preserved.  
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Binary Categorical Variables:} Two-level features (e.g., Yes/No, Male/Female) were dynamically encoded using mappings derived from the observed value set of each variable:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item[-] Semantic rules were applied e.g., (``no'', ``not'', ``female'' $\to 0.0$) and (``yes'', ``male'' $\to 1.0$).  
        \item[-] Count based detection verified that variables had exactly two non-missing unique values.  
        \item[-] Features with more than two sample values were also analysed for sentinel presence:
        \begin{itemize}
        \item[-] If a variable had three observed values and one was a sentinel, it was treated as binary.  
        \item[-] If a variable had four observed values and two were sentinels, it was treated as binary.  
        \end{itemize}
        \item[-] Sentinels were preserved as $\texttt{-1.0}$.  
        \item[-] This approach ensured order invariant encoding across waves, even when original labels or numeric prefixes differed.
        \item[-] \textit{Example (sentinel case):} Feature \texttt{fl002\_5} with pre transformed values \texttt{[`NOT Getting in or out of bed', -1.0, `Getting in or out of bed']} was encoded as \texttt{[0.0, -1.0, 1.0]}, demonstrating how a sentinel preserves binary semantics.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Multi-class / Mixed Variables (rule based feature engineering):} Variables with remaining multi-class structure, mixed types, or unresolved string numeric hybrids after earlier transformations were processed in the final step.

    Technical implementation details for this step are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:step6_code}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item[-] Lexical parsing extracted numbers, ranges, units, percentages, and textual frequency descriptors into floats, yielding interpretable numerical feature representations.  
        \item[-] Sentinel handling ensured all known non-response codes mapped to ($\texttt{-1.0}$).  
        \item[-] Original values were grouped and assigned numeric identifiers in first seen order, non numeric categorical labels were assigned integer codes above the highest observed numeric value to prevent collisions.  
        \item[-] Manual inspection using the Markdown audit file verified that semantic equivalence was maintained across waves.  
        \item[-] Step 6 was designed to be \emph{order invariant}, dynamically interpreting category values within each wave rather than relying on predefined lookup tables shared across waves. This preserved consistent numeric representation regardless of variable ordering or wave specific encodings.
        \begin{itemize}
        \item[-] \textit{Example:} Step 5 handled simple binary variables such as \texttt{CHRany\_pain} (any pain reported) with pre transformed values \texttt{[`pain', `No pain']} and numeric mapping \texttt{[1.0, 0.0]}. In contrast, Step 6 dynamically processed multi-class variables like \texttt{we114} (pre tax wage/salary) many of which contained over 20 distinct pre transformed categories (e.g., \texttt{-1.0, `200-299', `Less than 100', ..., `6,000-6,999'}) into consistent numeric representations (e.g., \texttt{-1.0, 249.5, 99, ..., 6499.5}). This illustrates why Step 6 required wave specific, dynamic parsing to maintain semantic equivalence across diverse observed values.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Feature engineering within the cleaning pipeline} \label{subsec:feature_engineering}
Although the six step pipeline is framed primarily as a numerical cleaning procedure, Steps 4--6 also perform explicit rule-based feature engineering by interpreting heterogeneous categorical survey responses and converting them into semantically meaningful numerical representations. Rather than acting as a separate post processing stage, feature engineering is embedded directly within the cleaning logic, formalising ordinal structure, frequency, and magnitude encoded in text into directly modelable \texttt{float64} values suitable for downstream analysis.

Crucially, this process engineers the \emph{representation} of each feature rather than expanding the feature space. For example, free text frequency responses such as ``\texttt{once or twice a week}'', ``\texttt{a couple of times per month}'', or ``\texttt{almost every day}'' are not treated as nominal categories. Instead, lexical cues (e.g., \emph{once}, \emph{twice}, \emph{couple}) are resolved to numeric quantities and normalised onto a common temporal scale, yielding a continuous rate representation. Implicit semantic information such as ordering, intensity, and frequency is therefore preserved through deterministic parsing and normalisation without introducing additional variables. This design maintains the original dimensionality of the dataset while ensuring numerical homogeneity and semantic integrity, producing a compact and model ready representation of complex real world survey data.

\subsection{Cross wave semantic verification}
Given the absence of guaranteed category ordering across waves, preserving semantic equivalence during numerical encoding and feature representation was essential to avoid introducing distortions into downstream \gls{ML} modelling. Pre and post transformation values for each group were exported to a readable Markdown (\texttt{.md}) file, as shown in Appendix~\ref{app:md_audit}. This representation confirmed that features of known importance for downstream predictive models were preserved and correctly encoded. For example, features such as \texttt{ph001} (self-rated general health), \texttt{ph009} (comparative self-rated health), and \texttt{bh006} (smoking frequency) correspond to items identified by \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} as among the most influential predictors in mortality models by the \gls{FI}. Retaining these features from the manual filtering thresholds applied in (Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter3}) and encoding them consistently across waves ensured that the cleaned dataset maintains coverage and semantic integrity, preventing distortions that could potentially compromise model performance.

A key challenge in cleaning the five \gls{TILDA} waves was the lack of guaranteed ordering for categorical values of the same variable across different waves. Table~\ref{tab:wave_ordering_example} illustrates this for Wave 4 and Wave 5.

For example, gender (\texttt{sex}) variables appeared under different value encodings, with varied category orderings and numeric prefixes across waves. Values such as \texttt{[`Male', `Female']}, \texttt{[`Female', `Male']}, and prefixed forms like \texttt{[``1.male', `2.female']} were all mapped to a consistent numeric representation (\texttt{1.0} for male, \texttt{0.0} for female). This ensured that semantic equivalence, rather than positional ordering or wave specific encoding, determined numerical assignment.

The variable \texttt{ph001} found in Appendix~\ref{app:md_audit} corresponds to the survey question: “Now I would like to ask you some questions about your health. Would you say your health is…”?. Responses are ordinal (Poor to Excellent) with non-response categories. 

The grouped Markdown representation reveals three critical points: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Semantically identical categories are correctly grouped even under different variable names (\texttt{ph001} vs \texttt{ph009}), enabling direct comparison of representations that would otherwise appear fragmented across waves. 
    \item Numerical encoding is invariant to category ordering, preserving ordinal meaning across waves. Without this approach, positional encoding would assign inconsistent values, potentially distorting performance for downstream models.
    \item For variables with a large and heterogeneous set of observed values (e.g., income bands, frequency ranges, or mixed numeric text responses), the grouped view provided essential visibility into how complex category spaces were being collapsed into stable numeric representations, supporting both validation and error detection at scale.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Validation of the Cleaning Pipeline}
The purpose of the cleaning validation stage was to assess whether the automated numerical parsing and encoding pipeline preserved meaningful biological and statistical structure within the cleaned datasets. Rather than asserting correctness based solely on successful execution, validation was performed by evaluating whether known relationships reported in the literature could be recovered using the cleaned data under realistic data governance constraints.

Due to restricted access to certain outcome variables within \gls{TILDA}, full end-to-end replication of all published models was not always feasible. In particular, access through the \texttt{Hotdesk Facility} for the official mortality outcomes is subject to additional governance requirements, including institutional \gls{GDPR} training certification and approval by the \gls{TILDA} data access committee \cite{HotDesksIrish}. 

As a result, validation focused on two complementary strategies: 
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item Internal consistency checks against established biological and clinical relationships, and 
\item Constrained replication of published modelling frameworks using available predictors and the derived \texttt{8y\_mortality\_proxy} outcome. 
\end{enumerate}
Together, these validation steps demonstrate that the cleaning pipeline retains expected relational patterns and produces datasets appropriate for downstream modelling tasks.

\subsection{Internal feature relationships}
The first validation stage assessed whether cleaned predictors preserved biologically and clinically plausible relationships reported in prior \gls{TILDA} studies. This analysis focused on predictors identified by \cite{davisLinearRegressionbasedMachine2021} as accounting for approximately $95\%$ of the explained variance in \gls{GSR}, defined as the difference between maximum and usual gait speed.

As \gls{GSR} is not directly available in the public Wave 3 dataset, validation was performed by examining the directional relationships between the 7 primary predictors themselves. This approach evaluates whether the numerical encoding process, including sentinel handling, missing value treatment, and ordinal mapping, retained the expected internal structure of the data rather than reproducing outcome specific performance.

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} presents the correlation matrix for the selected predictors following cleaning. Grip strength exhibited a negative association with both age and chair stand time, reflecting the inverse relationship between muscular strength and physical performance decline. Cognitive performance (\gls{MOCA}) demonstrated a moderate negative correlation with age and a positive association with educational level, consistent with known patterns of cognitive reserve in older adults. Education correlated positively with cognitive performance, and \gls{BMI} showed the expected weak negative association with age, reflecting reduced physiological reserve in older cohorts.

Table~\ref{tab:directional_validation} summarises the expected and observed directional relationships between key predictor pairs. Notably, grip strength showed moderate negative correlations with both age ($r=-0.265$) and chair stand time ($r=-0.170$), reflecting the expected inverse relationship between muscular strength and physical performance decline. Cognitive performance (\gls{MOCA}) displayed a moderate negative association with age ($r=-0.381$) and a positive association with education ($r=0.367$), consistent with known patterns of cognitive reserve in older adults. \gls{BMI} demonstrated the anticipated weak negative correlation with age ($r=-0.073$). These results confirm that the cleaning pipeline retained the expected numerical structure and relational patterns of the predictors, rather than introducing artificial associations.

\subsection{Model based validation}
To evaluate whether the cleaned dataset preserves predictive utility, a constrained replication of \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} was conducted. The resulting predictive performance for both model variants and sexes is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:model_replication}. The study predicted 8-year mortality using a 32-item \gls{FI}. In the cleaned Wave 1 dataset, only 16 of these 32 \gls{FI} items remained following the manual variable filtering described in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter3}, which prioritised global numeric consistency across waves.

A `\texttt{8y\_mortality\_proxy}' outcome was constructed to approximate the sex specific 8-year mortality rates reported in \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} (men: $14.9\%$, women: $11.1\%$) as observed via linkage to Ireland’s Central Register Office records \cite{wardLinkingDeathRegistration2020a}. Due to access restrictions, the official mortality data were unavailable; therefore, the proxy was created by randomly selecting deceased participants from the Wave 1 cohort aged 50 or older to match these percentages observed at Wave 5 follow up, and randomly sampling an equal number of alive participants to generate a balanced dataset for model training. The resulting datasets included 1,114 men (557 deceased) and 982 women (491 deceased), reflecting the baseline distribution of the Wave 1 participants ($\ge50$ years) = 8,172: 3,743 men and 4,429 women. This proxy serves a diagnostic role: although it does not replicate true mortality events, it allows evaluation of whether numerical relationships and predictive information are preserved in the cleaned data.

Two model variants were tested for each sex:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\gls{FI} only model:} using the 16 available \gls{FI} items, without age.
    \item \textbf{\gls{FI} $+$ Age model:} including age as an additional predictor.
\end{enumerate}
Model training and evaluation followed the procedure reported in \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} as closely as permitted by the available data. Features were min--max normalised prior to modelling. For each sex, data were split into training $(80\%)$ and testing $(20\%)$ sets. To mitigate overfitting and obtain robust estimates of model performance, 5-fold stratified cross-validation was applied on the training data. \gls{AUC} was selected as the primary evaluation metric due to its widespread use in prior \gls{TILDA} and \gls{ML} based studies (Figure~\ref{fig:prisma}) and its robustness to class imbalance.

For six \gls{FI} items (\texttt{mh0014}, \texttt{ph001}, \texttt{ph102}, \texttt{ph108}, \texttt{ph114}, \texttt{bh201}), the cleaned dataset transformations differed from the scoring rules published in \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021}. To align precisely with the original 16/32-item \gls{FI} methodology, these items were recoded following the published ordinal and dichotomous mappings. This step was necessary to ensure that the partially remapped \gls{FI} reflects the same interpretation of health deficits used in the literature and is consistent with standard \gls{FI} construction principles \cite{searleStandardProcedureCreating2008}. Models were then re-evaluated using this partial remapping \gls{FI} to assess sensitivity to feature encoding.

These results highlight several key points:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Despite relying on a reduced subset of the original 32 \gls{FI} items (16 available in the cleaned dataset), the \texttt{8y\_mortality\_proxy} models reflect expected trends. In particular, the \gls{AUC}s for the \gls{FI} only model (men: 0.507, women: 0.512) are lower than those reported by \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} (men: 0.70, women: 0.70), which is consistent with the use of a proxy mortality outcome for participants. Including age improves predictive performance (men: 0.516, women: 0.531), reproducing the pattern observed in the original study (men: 0.80, women: 0.90). The sex specific distributions \gls{FI} in our dataset (men: 0.2254–0.2258, women: 0.243–0.258 across alive/deceased) are broadly aligned with the published values (men: 0.11, women: 0.13).
    \item Re-mapping the six items to match the \cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021} \gls{FI} scoring yielded modest increases in \gls{AUC} scores (men: 0.567, women: 0.537 for \gls{FI} only; men: 0.572, women: 0.605 for \gls{FI} + age), illustrating that partial recoding restores some of the predictive information lost during initial variable filtering. This highlights the sensitivity of predictive performance to encoding choices and demonstrates that oversimplified $0-1$ scaling of metrics with scientifically constrained ranges, such as \gls{FI} where 0 indicates minimal deficits and 1 indicates maximal deficits, can distort interpretation and compromise the meaningfulness of derived scores.
    \item The cleaning pipeline successfully produced a fully numeric dataset suitable for downstream machine learning while maintaining sample characteristics close to the original cohort. Total participants over the age of 50 with a sex distribution of 45.8\% men and 54.2\% women, and expected deaths based on published rates of 14.9\% (men) and 11.1\% (women), confirming that the preprocessing and filtering preserved key demographic and outcome structures.
\end{enumerate}
