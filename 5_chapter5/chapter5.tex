\chapter{Empirical Evaluation}\label{ch:chapter5}

\section{Modelling and evaluation framework}
This section defines the modelling and evaluation setup used throughout this chapter, including model families and designs, analysis samples, outcome definitions, and evaluation procedures, building directly on the prepared datasets from Chapters~\ref{ch:chapter3} and~\ref{ch:chapter4}.

The evaluation is based on participants aged between $50$--$84$ years with a mean age of $66$. To ensure that the prediction task reflects incident (\emph{new onset}) disease, individuals with the target condition present at the start of a transition were excluded, leaving only \emph{at risk} observations for training and testing. This rule was applied consistently across all designs and for both Track A (Any disease outcome) and Track B (disease specific outcomes).

Final analysis samples differed by design and tracks due to data structure. The baseline design (D1) uses a single transition (Wave 1$\rightarrow$Wave 5), with a follow-up period of approximately 8~years. The pooled multi-wave designs (D2 and D3) aggregate all eligible consecutive transitions (W1$\rightarrow$W2, W2$\rightarrow$W3, W3$\rightarrow$W4, W4$\rightarrow$W5), each spanning approximately 2 years, thereby capturing shorter term dynamics. 

Sample sizes and incident rates are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:analysis_samples}. For Track B (disease specific), sample sizes vary by condition. Table (Appendix~incident\_rate\_PLACEHOLDER) provides the number of observations and incident rates for each disease specific model.

% -------- Outcome definitions -------- 
\subsection{Outcome definitions (Track A and Track B)}
Two complementary prediction tasks were defined.

\paragraph{Track A: Predicting any chronic disease onset.}\label{sc:track_a}
Track A uses a binary outcome indicating the first occurrence of any chronic disease during follow-up.
\begin{itemize}
    \item In D1, disease free status at Wave~1 was established using a composite of self-reported conditions (e.g., eye conditions (\texttt{ph105\_x}), cardiovascular conditions (\texttt{ph201\_xx}), diabetes related conditions (\texttt{ph216\_xx}), general chronic conditions (\texttt{ph301\_xx}), limiting long-term illness (\texttt{DISlongterm}), and self-reported chronic illness status (\texttt{ph003})). Incident cases were then defined using any positive \texttt{ICD10} codes from the \gls{WHO} \emph{International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems 10th Revision} at Wave~5 (e.g., mental and behavioural disorders (\texttt{ICD10\_05}), musculoskeletal diseases (\texttt{ICD10\_13})).
		\begin{itemize}
            \item No single \texttt{ICD10\_ANY} variable exists in Wave 1 to establish a clinical baseline. These clinical codes were recorded unevenly across the middle waves, with only three indicators available in Wave 2, two in Wave 3, and one in Wave 4, before a comprehensive set of 16 variables appeared at Wave 5. % Mention some where dropped form the manual filtering descirbed in chapter 3/4 optional sicne at teh start of tis chapter we did say continuing form ch3/4.
			\item PHYSICAL \& COGNITIVE HEALTH (PH) variables are self-reported responses that reflect prior doctor diagnoses (``has a doctor ever told you''), whereas \texttt{ph003} captures a general self-assessment of long term illness without requiring clinical confirmation
			\item All feature scoring schemes and labels are provided in Appendix~PLACEHOLDER.
    	\end{itemize}
    \item In D2 and D3, \texttt{ph003} was used to both define the outcome and identify the at risk cohort (\texttt{ph003}=0) at the start of each transition, as it is available in every wave and enables consistent application across all transitions. \[\text{Coverage}_{\text{ph003}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbbm{1}\big(\text{ph003}_{i} \neq missing(-1))}{N} \times 100 = 99.91\%\]
    \item An analysis of Wave 5 data compared self-reported illness (\texttt{ph003}) against clinical records (\texttt{ICD10\_ANY}). While raw agreement reached 59.5\%, the \emph{Cohen's Kappa for agreement beyond chance} (Kappa: 0.167) indicates poor consistency. This divergence reveals that 45\% of individuals who perceived themselves as healthy possessed an objective clinical diagnosis, while 38\% of those reporting illness had no corresponding hospital record. These results confirm that \texttt{ph003} serves as a distinct proxy for self-perceived chronic illness status. It captures a subjective signal of health burden that exists independently of formal clinical markers. % ToDo: drop claim - revise: justifying its use when predicting the presence of any chronic disease in the pooled transitions.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Track B: Disease specific prediction.}\label{sc:track_b}
Track B targets individual chronic disease outcomes, e.g., circulatory disease (\texttt{ICD10\_09}), diabetes (\texttt{ph726\_01}), and functional decline (\texttt{DISlongterm}). For each task, the outcome was defined as the first recorded diagnosis of the specific condition, and participants were excluded if the condition was present at the baseline of the corresponding transition. Scoring details for each disease specific outcome are provided in Appendix~PLACEHOLDER.

% -------- Modeling designs and hyperparameters --------
\subsection{Modelling designs}\label{sc:modelling_designs}
Four supervised learning algorithms were evaluated across all main Track A designs (D1, D2, D3) to compare linear and non-linear model families under the same evaluation framework.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\gls{LR}} --- A linear model providing interpretability through coefficients and odds ratios. In D1 and D2, a fixed configuration was used: \texttt{solver=``liblinear''}, \texttt{C=0.001}, \texttt{class\_weight=``balanced''},\\ \texttt{max\_iter=2000}. In D3, hyperparameters were tuned via \texttt{RandomizedSearchCV} over \texttt{C} (log-uniform $[10^{-4}, 10]$), \texttt{solver=[``saga'']}, \texttt{l1\_ratio} (uniform $[0,1]$), and \texttt{max\_iter} $\in \{1000, 2000, 5000\}$.
    \item \textbf{\gls{XGB}} --- Aka XGBoost, is a gradient boosted tree ensemble capturing non-linearities and feature interactions. In D1 and D2, fixed parameters were used: \texttt{n\_estimators=1500}, \texttt{max\_depth=3}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.03}, \texttt{min\_child\_weight=5}, \texttt{gamma=0.1}, \texttt{subsample=0.8}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree=0.7}, with class imbalance handled via \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight} (negative to positive ratio). Early stopping (\texttt{early\_stopping\_rounds=50}) on an internal 20\% validation split determined the optimal number of boosting rounds. The model was then refit on the full training set at that iteration. In D3, all tree and regularisation parameters were additionally tuned via \texttt{RandomizedSearchCV}.
    \item \textbf{\gls{RF}} --- A bagged ensemble of decision trees providing robustness through random feature subsampling (\texttt{max\_features=``sqrt''}). Fixed parameters in D1/D2: \texttt{n\_estimators=500}, \texttt{max\_depth=5}, \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf=10}, with class imbalance handled via \texttt{class\_weight=\{0:1, 1:\text{neg/pos ratio}\}}. In D3, \texttt{n\_estimators} (100--800), \texttt{max\_depth} (3--10), \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf} (5--30), and \texttt{max\_features} $\in \{$\texttt{``sqrt''}, \texttt{``log2''}$\}$ were tuned via random search.
    \item \textbf{\gls{MLP}} --- A feedforward neural network with two hidden layers capturing non-linear hierarchical representations. Fixed parameters in D1/D2: \texttt{hidden\_layer\_sizes=(32,16)}, \texttt{activation=``relu''}, \texttt{solver=``adam''}, \texttt{alpha=0.01}, \texttt{learning\_rate\_init=0.001}, \texttt{max\_iter=500}, with early stopping (\texttt{n\_iter\_no\_change=20}, \texttt{validation\_fraction=0.1}). Class imbalance was handled via \texttt{compute\_sample\_weight(class\_weight=\\``balanced'')} passed at fit time, as \gls{MLP} does not natively support \texttt{class\_weight}. In D3, \texttt{hidden\_layer\_sizes} $\in \{(32,), (64,), (32{,}16), (64{,}32), (128{,}64)\}$, \\\texttt{activation} $\in \{$\texttt{``relu''}, \texttt{``tanh''}$\}$, \texttt{alpha} (log-uniform $[10^{-4}, 1]$), and \\\texttt{learning\_rate\_init} (log-uniform $[10^{-4}, 10^{-1}]$) were tuned via random search.
\end{enumerate}

All four models shared the same preprocessing backbone: median imputation (\texttt{SimpleImputer}), variance filtering (\texttt{VarianceThreshold}, threshold = 0.01), and a missingness filter that dropped any feature exceeding 50\% missing values in the training set. Standard scaling (\texttt{StandardScaler}, zero mean and unit variance) was applied only for \gls{LR} and \gls{MLP}, which are sensitive to feature magnitude. Tree-based models (\gls{XGB} and \gls{RF}) were trained on the raw imputed features without scaling. All preprocessing steps were fit exclusively on the training portion (prior to internal train/validation splitting) before being applied to validation and test sets, preventing any information leakage.
 
Track B (disease specific) models used \gls{XGB} exclusively, as it achieved the strongest and most consistent performance across D2 and D3 in Track~A (see Section~\ref{sc:results}). The same model configuration and hyperparameter search space used in D3 was applied in Track~B.

The three primary modelling designs were implemented to address and assess the effect of baseline prediction, longitudinal pooling, and feature reduction with optimisation, respectively.
\begin{itemize}\label{three_designs}
\item[-] \textbf{D1 (Baseline):} A single W1$\rightarrow$W5 transition. Only participants disease free at Wave~1 (using self-reported and derived disease variables, plus explicit \texttt{ICD10} source variables) were included, with incident disease defined by any positive \texttt{ICD10} code at Wave~5 ($n=916$, incidence 27.1\%). Feature selection used \texttt{SelectKBest} ($k=20$) and \gls{CV} used \texttt{StratifiedKFold} (one observation per participant).
\item[-] \textbf{D2 (Multi-wave pooled):} All four eligible consecutive transitions (W1$\rightarrow$W2, W2$\rightarrow$W3, W3$\rightarrow$W4, W4$\rightarrow$W5) were stacked into a single pooled dataset. Disease free status and incident outcome were defined using \texttt{ph003} (self-reported chronic illness) at each transition. A numeric wave transition indicator (\texttt{wave\_transition\_num}) was added as a feature to capture temporal context. The pooled cohort comprised $n=14{,}296$ person wave observations (incidence 22.5\%). Feature selection used \texttt{SelectKBest} ($k=100$), and \gls{CV} used \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} to ensure no participant appeared in more than one fold.
\item[-] \textbf{D3 (Top--20 + \gls{HPO}):} Built directly on the D2 cohort and wave structure. The feature set was restricted to the 20 highest ranked predictors by \gls{XGB} gain importance from D2, plus \texttt{wave\_transition\_num}. No additional feature selection step was applied. \gls{HPO} was performed for all four model families using \texttt{RandomizedSearchCV} (200 iterations) within a \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} framework.
\end{itemize}
All three designs shared the same participant-level train/test split (80\%/20\%, stratified by outcome), the same missingness filter and variance threshold applied on training data only, and the same five fold \gls{CV} evaluation protocol. Track~B models followed either the D2 or D3 structure depending on the outcome being predicted. Full details are provided in Subsection~\ref{ssc:trackb}.

% -------- Data splitting and leakage prevention --------
\subsection{Participant-level data splitting and leakage prevention}
To prevent identity based leakage where models memorise participant specific health signatures rather than learning general risk patterns, all splitting was performed at the \textbf{participant level}. Individuals were assigned exclusively to either the training, validation, or test set, ensuring no participant contributed data to more than one split. The overall dataset was first split into 80\% for model development and 20\% as a held-out test set. The 80\% development partition was then further divided 80\%/20\% internally, yielding a final three way allocation of 64\% training, 16\% validation, and 20\% test. Splitting was performed using participant identifiers to ensure group wise separation, with stratification by outcome to preserve class balance across splits. Rumala~\cite{rumalaHowYouSplit2023c} demonstrates in longitudinal brain \gls{MRI} analysis, this subject wise approach prevents overconfident performance and identity confounding, where models learn individual characteristics alongside diagnostic features, ensuring generalisation to truly unseen individuals.

For D1, where each participant contributes exactly one observation, standard \texttt{StratifiedKFold} ($k=5$) was used. For D2 and D3, where participants may appear across multiple wave transitions, \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} ($k=5$) was used to ensure that all observations from a given participant were assigned to the same fold during \gls{CV}, preventing within participant data from appearing in both training and validation folds simultaneously.

Three additional leakage controls were applied across all designs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pipeline isolation:} Imputation medians, variance masks, and feature selection scores were computed on the training partition only. The resulting transformations were then applied unchanged to validation and test data.
    \item \textbf{Temporal ordering:} Predictors were drawn exclusively from the baseline wave of each transition (Wave~$n$) to predict outcomes at the subsequent wave (Wave~$n+1$), eliminating any look ahead bias.
    \item \textbf{Exclusion of direct proxies:} Variables that directly encode the outcome being predicted (e.g., self-reported disease source indicators that feed into the \texttt{ICD10} codes used as outcome, such as \texttt{ph201\_07} and \texttt{ph301\_06} for nervous system diseases (ICD10\_06)), were removed from the predictor set prior to modelling.
\end{itemize}

% -------- Evaluation protocol and metrics --------
\subsection{Evaluation protocol and decision metrics}
Model performance was assessed using a combination of discrimination, calibration, and classification metrics. Models were trained by minimising their respective objective (loss) functions, while performance was evaluated using out-of-sample metrics. The primary metric \gls{AUC}, chosen for its robustness to class imbalance and its interpretability in risk prediction contexts. Model performance was estimated using 5-fold \gls{CV} on the training set. For D2 and D3, this was implemented using \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} to ensure participant-level separation across folds, while D1 used \texttt{StratifiedKFold}. Models that required \gls{HPO} (D3), a random search (\texttt{RandomizedSearchCV}) was conducted within the same \gls{CV} framework.

Calibration was evaluated using the Brier score, which measures the accuracy of predicted probabilities, while classification performance was summarised using the F1 score, capturing the balance between precision and recall in the presence of class imbalance (e.g., 22--27\% incidence). Additional metrics, including sensitivity, specificity, and confusion matrices, were used to support interpretation.

For Track B predictor add-on analysis, 95\% confidence intervals for performance deltas were estimated via bootstrap resampling to ensure statistical significance.

\subsection{Cross-design comparison}
The best test \gls{AUC} increased from $0.582$ in D1~\ref{ssc:d1} (achieved by \gls{MLP}) to $0.715$ in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} by \gls{XGB}, representing an improvement of $0.133$, demonstrating the benefit of incorporating pooled longitudinal transitions.

Reducing the feature set to the top 20 predictors in D3 maintained this gain, with \gls{XGB} achieving a test \gls{AUC} of $0.713$, only $0.002$ lower than D2, confirming that most of the predictive signal is concentrated in a small subset of variables, and that performance is robust to feature reduction.

Across designs, \gls{CV} performance also improves and stabilises:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] D1: $0.589 \pm 0.072$ (\gls{LR})
    \item[-] D2: $0.716 \pm 0.007$ (\gls{XGB})
    \item[-] D3: $0.718 \pm 0.006$ (\gls{XGB})
\end{itemize}

The reduction in standard deviation from D1 to D2 and D3 reflects improved stability associated with the pooled multi-wave design and more consistent learning signal across transitions, although differences in data structure across designs should be considered when interpreting cross-design variability.

\subsection{Model comparison}
Once temporal structure is introduced (D2 and D3), differences between model families are modest. Test \gls{AUC} values cluster in the range ($0.69$--$0.72$), with no single model class dominating performance. \gls{XGB} achieved the highest test \gls{AUC} in both D2 ($0.715$) and D3 ($0.713$), though the margin over \gls{LR} was narrow ($\leq 0.006$). The fact that the linear \gls{LR} remained consistently competitive ($0.709$ in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} and $0.712$ in D3~\ref{ssc:d3}) suggests that risk signals in the ageing population are primarily linear. \gls{MLP} slightly underperforms in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} ($0.699$) but matched other models in D3~\ref{ssc:d3} ($0.712$), indicating improved performance when the feature space is reduced to the most informative predictors. This suggests that individual health indicators, such as physical pain (\texttt{ph501}) or medication counts (\texttt{MDmeds4}), contribute largely and independently to the risk score, with limited additional benefit from modelling complex non-linear interactions.

Figure~\ref{fig:generalisation_gap} compares generalisation gaps (Train $-$ Test) \gls{AUC} across all three designs. The average train-test \gls{AUC} gap (across all four models) drops from $0.202$ in D1 to $0.030$ in D2 and $0.028$ in D3 --- a reduction of over $85\%$. This demonstrates that the pooled multi-wave design effectively reduces overfitting.
D1 exhibits the largest gaps, consistent with weaker signal and higher variance in estimates. In contrast, D2 and D3 show much smaller gaps, indicating improved stability. Tree based models (\gls{XGB} and \gls{RF}) exhibit the largest gaps in D1, consistent with the small sample size. In D2 and D3, \gls{XGB} achieves the smallest gaps ($0.021$ and $0.023$ respectively), while \gls{RF} shows comparatively larger gaps ($0.029$ and $0.053$). The strong performance of \gls{XGB} in D3 is expected, as the top-20 feature set was selected using \gls{XGB} gain importance from D2. Nevertheless, \gls{XGB} also generalises well in D2, suggesting that its boosting mechanism is effective at learning stable patterns from pooled longitudinal data. In contrast, \gls{RF} may be more prone to overfitting even with the pooled design, possibly due to its reliance on bagging and random feature selection, which may capture informative predictors less efficiently than boosting.
