\chapter{Empirical Evaluation}\label{ch:chapter5}

\section{Modelling and evaluation framework}
This section defines the modelling and evaluation setup used throughout this chapter, including model families and designs, analysis samples, outcome definitions, and evaluation procedures, building directly on the prepared datasets from Chapters~\ref{ch:chapter3} and~\ref{ch:chapter4}.

The evaluation is based on participants aged between 50--80 years with a mean age of 57.8. To ensure that the task reflects incident \emph{new onset} disease, individuals with the target condition present at the start of a transition were excluded, leaving only \emph{at risk} observations for training and testing. This rule was applied consistently across all designs and for both Track A (composite outcome) and Track B (disease specific outcomes).

Final analysis samples differed by design and tracks due to data structure. The baseline design (D1) uses a single transition (Wave 1$\rightarrow$Wave 5), a follow-up period of approximately 8~years. The pooled multi-wave designs (D2 and D3) aggregate all eligible consecutive transitions (W1$\rightarrow$W2, W2$\rightarrow$W3, W3$\rightarrow$W4, W4$\rightarrow$W5), each spanning approximately 2 years, thereby capturing shorter term dynamics. 

Sample sizes and incident rates are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:analysis_samples}. For Track B (disease specific), sample sizes vary by condition. An appendix table (Appendix~incident\_rate\_PLACEHOLDER) provides the number of observations and incident rates for each disease specific model.

\subsection{Outcome definitions (Track A and Track B)}
Two complementary prediction tasks were defined.

\paragraph{Track A: Predicting any chronic disease onset.}\label{sc:track_a}
Track A uses a binary outcome indicating the first occurrence of any chronic disease during follow-up.
\begin{itemize}
    \item In D1, the outcome was derived from a composite of self reported conditions recorded in Wave 1 (e.g., eye conditions (\texttt{ph105\_x}), cardiovascular conditions (\texttt{ph201\_xx}), diabetes related conditions (\texttt{ph216\_xx}), general chronic conditions (\texttt{ph301\_xx}), limiting long term illness (\texttt{DISlongterm}), and self reported chronic illness status (\texttt{ph003})). Incident cases were defined using \texttt{ICD10\_ANY} from the \gls{WHO} \emph{International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems 10th Revision} in Wave 5 (e.g., mental and behavioural disorders (\texttt{ICD10\_05}), musculoskeletal diseases (\texttt{ICD10\_13})). 
		\begin{itemize}
			\item No single \texttt{ICD10\_ANY} variable exists in Wave 1 to establish a clinical baseline. These clinical codes were recorded unevenly across the middle waves, with only three indicators available in Wave 2, two in Wave 3, and one in Wave 4, before a comprehensive set of 16 variables appeared at Wave 5.
			\item PHYSICAL \& COGNITIVE HEALTH (PH) variables are self reported responses that reflect prior doctor diagnoses (“has a doctor ever told you”), whereas \texttt{ph003} captures a general self-assessment of long term illness without requiring clinical confirmation
			\item All feature scoring schemes and labels are provided in Appendix~PLACEHOLDER.
    	\end{itemize}
    \item In D2 and D3, \texttt{ph003} was used to both define the outcome and identify the at risk cohort (\texttt{ph003}=0) at the start of each transition, as it is available in every wave and enables consistent application across all transitions.
    \item An analysis of Wave 5 data compared self reported illness (\texttt{ph003}) against clinical records (\texttt{ICD10\_ANY}). While raw agreement reached 59.5\%, the \emph{Cohen's Kappa for agreement beyond chance} (Kappa: 0.167) indicates poor consistency. This divergence reveals that 45\% of individuals who perceived themselves as healthy possessed an objective clinical diagnosis, while 38\% of those reporting illness had no corresponding hospital record. These results confirm that \texttt{ph003} serves as a distinct proxy for self perceived chronic illness status. It captures a subjective signal of health burden that exists independently of formal clinical markers, justifying its use when predicting the presence of any chronic disease in the pooled transitions.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Track B: Disease specific prediction.}\label{sc:track_b}
Track B targets individual chronic disease outcomes, e.g., circulatory disease (\texttt{ICD10\_09}), diabetes (\texttt{ph726\_01}), and functional decline (\texttt{DISlongterm}). For each task, the outcome was defined as the first recorded diagnosis of the specific condition, and participants were excluded if the condition was present at the baseline of the corresponding transition. Coding details for each disease specific outcome are provided in Appendix~PLACEHOLDER.

\subsection{Modelling designs}\label{sc:modelling_designs}
Four supervised learning algorithms were evaluated across all main Track A designs (D1, D2, D3) to compare linear and non-linear model families under the same evaluation framework.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\gls{LR}} – a linear baseline model capturing linear effects and providing interpretability through coefficients and odds ratios, with regularisation (e.g., L1/Lasso) to mitigate overfitting in high dimensional settings.
    \item \textbf{\gls{XGB}} – a tree based gradient boosted model that captures non-linearities and interactions, with built in regularisation to mitigate overfitting and feature importance measures to identify key predictors.
    \item \textbf{\gls{RF}} – a bagged ensemble of decision trees that captures complex patterns and is robust to noise and outliers through random feature selection and averaging over trees, with feature importance measures based on mean decrease in impurity or permutation importance.
    \item \textbf{\gls{MLP}} – a feedforward neural network that captures complex non-linear relationships through multiple layers of abstraction, with regularisation techniques (e.g., dropout) to prevent overfitting and the ability to learn hierarchical feature representations, with reduced interpretability compared to tree based models.
\end{enumerate}

The three primary modelling designs were implemented to address and assess the effect of baseline only prediction, pooled longitudinal prediction, and feature reduction, respectively.
\begin{itemize}\label{three_designs}
\item[-] \textbf{D1 (Baseline):} Single transition from Wave 1 to Wave 5. Only participants free of the outcome at Wave 1 were included. Feature selection: SelectKBest ($k=20$). All models were trained with default hyperparameters to establish a baseline performance for each model family.
\item[-] \textbf{D2 (Multi-wave pooled):} All eligible consecutive transitions (W1$\rightarrow$W2, W2$\rightarrow$W3, W3$\rightarrow$W4, W4$\rightarrow$W5) stacked together. A wave transition indicator was included as a feature. Feature selection: SelectKBest ($k=100$) applied to the pooled dataset.
\item[-] \textbf{D3 (Top-20 features):} Same pooled structure as D2, but restricted to the 20 most informative predictors identified by \gls{XGB} gain importance from D2. No additional feature selection was applied in D3 to allow the models to leverage the same reduced feature set. Hyperparameter optimisation was performed for all models in D3 using grid search within the same \gls{CV} framework to ensure a fair comparison of optimised performance across model families.
\end{itemize}
Track B (disease specific) models followed either the D2 or D3 design, with \gls{XGB} as the primary model family because it performed best in Track A (D2/D3). Each design was chosen to address specific research questions: D1 for baseline prediction, D2 for leveraging longitudinal information, and D3 for evaluating the impact of feature reduction with model hyperparameter optimisation.

Before model training, features with more than 50\% missing values were removed to reduce sparsity. Remaining missing values were handled using median imputation. All model input pipeline steps, including feature filtering and imputation, were fitted on the training data only and then applied unchanged to validation and test sets to prevent information leakage.

This ensured that no information from held out data influenced feature engineering or model fitting. To account for class imbalance, model specific weighting strategies were applied: balanced class weights for \gls{LR} and \gls{RF}, scale\_pos\_weight for \gls{XGB}, and sample weights for \gls{MLP}. Standard scaling (zero mean, unit variance) was applied only to the features used by \gls{LR} and \gls{MLP}. Tree based models (XGB and RF) were trained on the raw imputed features without scaling. For D3, hyperparameter optimisation was performed using grid search within \gls{CV}. For example, \gls{XGB} was limited to a maximum depth of 3, and random forest used a maximum depth of 5 with a minimum of 10 samples per leaf.

\subsection{Participant level data splitting and leakage prevention}
To prevent identity based leakage where models memorise participant specific health signatures rather than learning general risk patterns, all splitting was performed at the \textbf{participant level}. Individuals were assigned exclusively to either the training, validation, or test set, ensuring no participant contributed data to more than one split. As Rumala~\cite{rumalaHowYouSplit2023c} demonstrates in longitudinal brain \gls{MRI} analysis, this subject wise approach prevents overconfident performance and identity confounding, where models learn individual characteristics alongside diagnostic features, ensuring generalisation to truly unseen individuals.

Additional leakage prevention measures included:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pipeline isolation:} All pipeline steps (median imputation, scaling, SelectKBest) were fitted using training data only and then applied to validation and test sets.
    \item \textbf{Temporal ordering:} Predictors were drawn only from baseline (start of a transition) \emph{Wave n} predicts outcomes at follow-up \emph{Wave n+1} eliminating look ahead bias.
    \item \textbf{Exclusion of direct proxies:} When predicting a specific disease (e.g., hypertension), variables that directly indicated the same condition (e.g., \texttt{ph201\_01}) were removed from the predictor set.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation protocol and decision metrics}
Model performance was assessed using a combination of discrimination, calibration, and classification metrics. The primary metric was the \gls{AUC}, chosen for its robustness to class imbalance and its interpretability in risk prediction contexts. Model performance was estimated using 5-fold stratified \gls{CV} on the training set, which was also used as the internal resampling strategy for model comparison across all designs. For models that required hyperparameter optimisation (D3), a grid search was conducted within the same \gls{CV} framework.

Calibration was evaluated using the Brier score, which measures the accuracy of predicted probabilities, while classification performance was summarised using the F1 score, capturing the balance between precision and recall in the presence of class imbalance (e.g., 22--27\% incidence). Additional metrics, including sensitivity, specificity, and confusion matrices, were used to support interpretation.

For the Track B add-on analysis, 95\% confidence intervals for performance deltas were estimated via bootstrap resampling to ensure statistical significance.

\subsection{Cross-design comparison}
The best test \gls{AUC} increased from $0.582$ in D1~\ref{ssc:d1} (achieved by \gls{MLP}) to $0.715$ in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} by \gls{XGB}, representing an improvement of $0.133$, demonstrating the benefit of incorporating pooled longitudinal transitions.

Reducing the feature set to the top 20 predictors in D3 maintained this gain, with \gls{XGB} achieving a test \gls{AUC} of $0.713$, only $0.002$ lower than D2, confirming that most of the predictive signal is concentrated in a small subset of variables, and that performance is robust to feature reduction.

Across designs, \gls{CV} performance also improves and stabilises:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] D1: $0.589 \pm 0.072$ (\gls{LR})
    \item[-] D2: $0.716 \pm 0.007$ (\gls{XGB})
    \item[-] D3: $0.718 \pm 0.006$ (\gls{XGB})
\end{itemize}

The reduction in standard deviation from D1 to D2 and D3 reflects improved stability associated with the pooled multi-wave design and more consistent learning signal across transitions, although differences in data structure across designs should be considered when interpreting cross design variability.

\subsection{Model comparison}
Once temporal structure is introduced (D2 and D3), differences between model families are modest. Test \gls{AUC}s cluster in the range $(0.699$--$0.715)$, with no single model class dominating performance. \gls{XGB} achieved the highest test \gls{AUC} in both D2~\ref{ssc:d2} ($0.715$) and D3~\ref{ssc:d3} ($0.713$), though the margin over \gls{LR} was narrow ($\leq 0.006$). The fact that the linear \gls{LR} remained consistently competitive ($0.709$ in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} and $0.712$ in D3~\ref{ssc:d3}) suggests that risk signals in the ageing population are primarily linear. \gls{MLP} slightly underperforms in D2~\ref{ssc:d2} ($0.699$) but matched other models in D3~\ref{ssc:d3} ($0.712$), indicating improved performance when the feature space is reduced to the most informative predictors. This suggests that individual health indicators, such as physical pain (\texttt{ph501}) or medication counts (\texttt{MDmeds4}), contribute largely and independently to the risk score, with limited additional benefit from modelling complex non-linear interactions.

Figure~\ref{fig:generalisation_gap} compares generalisation gaps (Train - Test \gls{AUC}s) across all three designs. The average train--test \gls{AUC} gap (across all four models) drops from $0.202$ in D1 to $0.030$ in D2 and $0.028$ in D3 – a reduction of over $85\%$. This demonstrates that the pooled multi-wave design effectively reduces overfitting.
D1 exhibits the largest gaps, consistent with weaker signal and higher variance in estimates. In contrast, D2 and D3 show much smaller gaps, indicating improved stability. While tree based models (\gls{XGB} and \gls{RF}) exhibit the largest gaps in the small sample size of D1. However, D2 and D3, \gls{XGB} achieves the smallest gaps ($0.021$ and $0.023$ respectively), while \gls{RF} still shows comparatively larger gaps ($0.029$ and $0.053$). The strong performance of \gls{XGB} in D3 is expected, as the top-20 feature set was selected using \gls{XGB} gain importance from D2. Nevertheless, \gls{XGB} also generalises well in D2, suggesting that its boosting mechanism is effective at learning stable patterns from pooled longitudinal data. In contrast, \gls{RF} may be more prone to overfitting even with the pooled design, possibly due to its reliance on bagging and random feature selection, which may capture informative predictors less efficiently than boosting.
