\section{Summary}
This thesis developed and evaluated a longitudinal \gls{ML} pipeline for predicting chronic disease onset in older adults using all five waves of \gls{TILDA} data. Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter3} described the study population and manual variable screening workflow, which reduced over 5,000 raw features to a structured, domain-organised dataset. Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter4} presented the deterministic six-step cleaning pipeline and validated the transformed features against published benchmarks. Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5} evaluated four model families across three modelling designs and 24 disease specific outcomes, finding that pooled multi-wave designs substantially outperformed the single-wave baseline, that predictive signal concentrated in a small cluster of health burden indicators, and that no domain specific add-on feature contributed statistically significant independent predictive value.

\section{Conclusions}
% DRAFT
One of the more striking findings of this study concerns the gap between the variety of data available and the narrowness of what turned out to matter. Of the $3{,}099$ features retained after manual screening (Table~\ref{tab:feature_groups}), covering sociodemographic context, physical health, functional status, lifestyle, nutrition, and psychological wellbeing, the 20 predictors that drove nearly all discriminative signal came from just two of those domains, physical health and functional status. This points to something worth reflecting on: self-rated health, pain, and medication use are not just convenient proxies for disease risk. In survey cohorts like \gls{TILDA}, they appear to act as integrative markers, absorbing the accumulated effect of social circumstances, lifestyle, psychological state, and biological vulnerability that might otherwise require hundreds of individual variables to capture separately. Whether that combined signal can be disaggregated into clinically specific components is a question for future work that requires richer data than the publicly available release provides.

\subsection{Data availability} 
This analysis was conducted using the publicly accessible \gls{TILDA} release (waves~1--5), a pseudonymised dataset covering self-reported health, physical, cognitive, nutritional, and behavioural variables, together with ICD-10 coded disease indicators from wave~2 onwards. The datasets are accessible through the \gls{ISSDA} at \url{https://tilda.tcd.ie/data/accessing-data/}.

Several data sources relevant to chronic disease prediction are not included in the public release. These require either on-site access at \gls{TILDA}'s Trinity College Dublin facility or remote access through the \gls{TILDA} VISTA trusted research environment. They include a dedicated biomarkers dataset, the wave~3 \gls{MRI} dataset, an epigenetics dataset, linked primary care records, and linked mortality records. Access to these sources would enable future work to distinguish more precisely between perceived health burden, objective physiological decline, and verified disease trajectories.

\section{Future Work}
The recently released sixth wave of \gls{TILDA} data (December 2025), together with the Intellectual Disability Supplement and the COVID-19 sub-study, would extend the longitudinal coverage and introduce health trajectory data spanning a pandemic period, likely altering self-reported burden profiles in meaningful ways. Cross-cohort validation using the \gls{RAND} harmonised \gls{TILDA} dataset, which maps variables to international standards compatible with cohorts such as \gls{HRS} and \gls{SHARE}, would test how well the identified health burden cluster generalises beyond the Irish context. Finally, access to the restricted biomarker panels, primary care records, and linked mortality data held within \gls{TILDA}'s secure research environment would allow future work to test whether the signal limits identified here reflect the nature of chronic disease risk itself or the constraints of the publicly available dataset.
