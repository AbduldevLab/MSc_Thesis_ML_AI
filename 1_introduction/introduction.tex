Ageing populations experience substantial changes across physical, nutritional, cognitive, and psychosocial domains, each shaping vulnerability to chronic disease and functional decline~\cite{davisComparisonGaitSpeed2021, mooreCountingTimeLived2017, huangSexDifferencesAssociations2020}. Understanding how these multidomain factors interact over time is essential for early risk identification.

The financial and societal burden is substantial. In Europe, poor diet is a major risk factor for non communicable diseases, contributing billions in healthcare costs annually~\cite{WhoNutritionReport}. In Ireland, malnutrition alone is estimated to cost over €1.4 billion per year~\cite{RICE2012}. In response to these challenges, international public health frameworks, including the \gls{WHO} \emph{Global Action Plan} on noncommunicable diseases and Ireland's \emph{Healthy Ireland} framework, highlight nutrition and lifestyle factors as central components of disease prevention strategies.

European regulatory frameworks such as the \gls{GDPR} and proposed \gls{EU} \gls{AI} Act emphasise transparency and highlight the need for interpretable \gls{ML} models in healthcare.

Traditional risk prediction models have largely relied on clinical biomarkers such as \gls{BP} and lipid profiles. Yet, growing evidence shows that broader predictors, including nutrition~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023}, \gls{PA}~\cite{lairdPhysicalActivityDose2023}, \gls{MH}~\cite{mcdowellAssociationsPhysicalActivity2018}, and \gls{SOC}~\cite{mccrorySocialDisadvantageSocial2016} improve predictive performance. Estimating the likelihood of developing a condition given certain risk factors helps quantify the influence of these predictors in clinical decision making. 

\gls{TILDA}\footnote{\gls{TILDA}'s dataset are pseudonymised and accessed under license via \gls{ISSDA}. Use adheres to \gls{UL} S\&E ethics approval and data retention requirements.} provides a national representative cohort of adults aged $\texttt{50+}$ in Ireland, surveyed repeatedly across multiple domains. For cross study comparability and methodological consistency in model development, selected variables from the externally produced \gls{RAND} Harmonised \gls{TILDA} dataset (available for Waves 1 and 2) were included. 

The harmonised dataset provides standardised variable definitions and documented construction rules, which are particularly relevant in a machine learning setting where models are trained and evaluated on partitioned data. Its inclusion supports robust feature engineering, reproducible train validation splits, and alignment with prior ageing related \gls{ML} studies that adopt harmonised frameworks. The specific role of harmonised variables within the modelling pipeline is detailed in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter3}.

Despite these advantages, existing \gls{ML} research on chronic disease prediction faces persistent limitations: over reliance on clinical predictors, limited incorporation of lifestyle and \gls{SOC} variables, predominantly cross sectional study designs that fail to capture trajectories, and a lack of interpretability consistent with emerging \gls{AI} governance standards.  

This thesis addresses these gaps by leveraging longitudinal \gls{TILDA} data across multiple domains, using interpretable \gls{ML} methods to combine modifiable health behaviours, psychosocial factors, and established clinical measures to enhance prediction of ageing related outcomes.

\section{Research problem and motivation} 

Despite increasing research and clinical interest in lifestyle, nutrition factors, and advances in \gls{ML} methods, approaches to chronic disease prediction in older adults remain limited in several ways.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limited Integration of Multidomain Predictors:} Existing models focus primarily on clinical measurements and often neglect modifiable behavioural, dietary, and psychosocial factors~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023, lairdPhysicalActivityDepression2023}.
    \item \textbf{Underuse of Longitudinal Dynamics:} Few studies leverage repeated measures to capture developments of risk, such as \gls{FR} progression~\cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021}.
    Probabilistic reasoning can help estimate the likelihood of developing a condition at a future time given earlier measurements, for example, calculating the probability $P(\text{\gls{FR} at wave 5} \mid \text{\gls{UGS} at wave 1}, \text{Nutrition at wave 2})$.

    This approach helps quantify how early indicators influence later outcomes and supports better feature selection and timing of interventions in predictive models.
    \item \textbf{Lack of Explainability:}Many \gls{ML} models are not interpretable, limiting clinical adoption and failing to meet emerging regulatory requirements for trustworthy \gls{AI} in healthcare~\cite{donoghueUsingConditionalInference2023}.
\end{itemize}
Addressing these challenges is essential to improve predictive accuracy, support evidence based interventions, and ensure alignment with evolving policy and regulatory priorities.

\section{Research aims and objectives}

The goal of this thesis is to develop and validate \gls{ML} models that integrate nutrition, lifestyle, and \gls{MH} features with clinical data to improve prediction of chronic disease and ageing related outcomes in a representative cohort of older adults. This goal will be achieved through the following objectives:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[-] Review existing applications in \gls{ML} for chronic disease prediction in ageing populations, with emphasis on gaps in lifestyle and nutrition integration.
  \item[-] Develop a reproducible pipeline to preprocess and align \gls{TILDA} waves 1--5, and engineer multidomain features (nutrition, activity, mobility, \gls{MH}, clinical indicators).
  \item[-] Develop and compare baseline statistical models (e.g., logistic regression) with advanced interpretable \gls{ML} methods such as \gls{RF} and \gls{GBM}.
  \item[-] Evaluate models using predictive classification performance metrics including \gls{AUC}, calibration and error measures (accuracy, precision, \gls{recall}, \gls{F1}), perform ablation analyses to assess the incremental value of lifestyle and nutrition predictors.
  \item[-] Identify actionable predictors (e.g., vitamin D status, \gls{UGS} reserve) and highlight modifiable risk factors for intervention.
  \item[-] Document ethical, governance, and reproducibility practices for handling longitudinal pseudonymised health data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Contributions of the research}

This research makes contributions at three levels:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Empirical:} Demonstrates how behavioural, dietary, and other \gls{MH} features complement established clinical measures to enhance predictive performance.
  \item \textbf{Methodological:} Provides a reproducible pipeline for aligning longitudinal \gls{TILDA} data across domains and integrating diverse features into \gls{ML} workflows.  
  \item \textbf{Clinical:} Highlights actionable modifiable factors alongside traditional clinical indicators for targeted interventions and policy. 
\end{itemize}

\section{Thesis outline}

\emph{Chapter}~\ref{ch:chapter2} presents a literature review on \gls{ML} prediction of chronic disease in ageing populations, focusing on lifestyle, nutrition predictors and methodological considerations.
\emph{Chapter}~\ref{ch:chapter3} describes the data preparation process, including the \gls{TILDA} study design, data collection, thematic domain structure, and the manual variable filtering workflow.
In \emph{Chapter}~\ref{ch:chapter4}, the automated cleaning pipeline is presented, including the variable scenario detection system, the deterministic six-step cleaning sequence, and validation of the pipeline against established benchmarks.
\emph{Chapter 5} presents the experimental results, including model comparisons and feature importance analysis. 
\emph{Chapter 6} discusses the implications and future directions of the research.
