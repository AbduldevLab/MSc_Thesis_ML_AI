Ageing populations experience substantial changes across physical, nutritional, cognitive, and psychosocial domains, each shaping vulnerability to chronic disease and functional decline~\cite{davisComparisonGaitSpeed2021, mooreCountingTimeLived2017, huangSexDifferencesAssociations2020}. Understanding how these multidomain factors interact over time is essential for early risk identification.

Lifestyle risk factors contribute billions in healthcare costs annually across Europe~\cite{WhoNutritionReport}, and in Ireland, malnutrition alone is estimated to cost over €1.4 billion per year~\cite{RICE2012}. Frameworks such as the \gls{WHO} \emph{Global Action Plan} and Ireland's \emph{Healthy Ireland} strategy identify modifiable health behaviours as central to disease prevention.

European regulatory frameworks such as the \gls{GDPR} and proposed \gls{EU} \gls{AI} Act emphasise transparency and highlight the need for interpretable \gls{ML} models in healthcare.

Traditional risk prediction models have largely relied on clinical biomarkers such as \gls{BP} and lipid profiles. Yet, growing evidence shows that broader predictors, including nutrition~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023}, \gls{PA}~\cite{lairdPhysicalActivityDose2023}, \gls{MH}~\cite{mcdowellAssociationsPhysicalActivity2018}, and \gls{SOC}~\cite{mccrorySocialDisadvantageSocial2016} improve predictive performance.

\gls{TILDA}\footnote{\gls{TILDA}'s data are pseudonymised and accessed under license via \gls{ISSDA}. Use adheres to \gls{UL} Science \& Engineering ethics approval and data retention requirements.} provides a nationally representative cohort of adults aged 50 and over in Ireland, surveyed repeatedly across physical, clinical, functional, psychological, and social domains. An externally produced \gls{RAND} harmonised \gls{TILDA} dataset (waves~1--2), aligned to international standards, was inspected and preprocessed alongside the five core waves.

Despite these advantages, existing \gls{ML} research on chronic disease prediction faces persistent limitations: over reliance on clinical predictors, limited incorporation of lifestyle and \gls{SOC} variables, predominantly cross sectional study designs that fail to capture risk trajectories, and insufficient attention to the preprocessing and representation of heterogeneous longitudinal health data.

This thesis addresses these gaps by applying \gls{ML} to pooled and cross-wave \gls{TILDA} data, evaluating whether richer predictor sets, longitudinal designs, and reproducible preprocessing improve chronic disease onset prediction in older adults.

\section{Research problem and motivation} 
Despite increasing research and clinical interest in lifestyle, nutritional factors, and advances in \gls{ML} methods, approaches to chronic disease prediction in older adults remain limited in several ways.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limited integration of multidomain predictors:} Existing models focus primarily on clinical measurements and often neglect modifiable behavioural, dietary, and psychosocial factors~\cite{murphyPlasmaLuteinZeaxanthin2023, lairdPhysicalActivityDepression2023}.
    \item \textbf{Underuse of longitudinal dynamics:} Few studies use repeated measures to track how risk changes over time, such as the progression of \gls{FR}~\cite{moguilnerImportanceAgePrediction2021}.
    Probabilistic reasoning can help estimate the likelihood of developing a condition at a future time given earlier measurements, for example, calculating the probability $P(\text{\gls{FR} at wave 5} \mid \text{\gls{UGS} at wave 1}, \text{Nutrition at wave 2})$.
    \item \textbf{Healthcare data preprocessing:} Longitudinal health survey data present significant representation challenges. Variables encoding the same concept may differ in format, value ordering, and coding scheme across waves, while health specific constructs such as frailty index items require careful ordinal handling to preserve clinical meaning during numerical conversion~\cite{searleStandardProcedureCreating2008}. These challenges are rarely reported in the \gls{ML} literature, yet the quality of downstream predictions depends directly on how accurately the data are prepared.
\end{itemize}
These gaps motivate the multi-wave, multidomain approach taken in this thesis, which uses \gls{TILDA}'s five longitudinal waves to test whether richer predictor sets and pooled designs improve chronic disease onset prediction.

\section{Research aims and objectives}
The goal of this thesis is to develop and evaluate longitudinal \gls{ML} approaches that combine multidomain predictors from \gls{TILDA} to predict chronic disease onset in older adults, while identifying the features and modelling designs that best support this task. 

This goal will be achieved through the following objectives:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[-] Review existing applications in \gls{ML} for chronic disease prediction in ageing populations, with emphasis on gaps in lifestyle and nutrition integration.
  \item[-] Develop a reproducible pipeline to preprocess and align \gls{TILDA} waves~1--5, deriving multidomain features across physical, functional, clinical, lifestyle, and psychosocial domains.
  \item[-] Develop and compare a linear baseline (\gls{LR}) against tree ensemble and neural network families (\gls{XGB}, \gls{RF}, \gls{MLP}) across single wave and pooled longitudinal designs.
  \item[-] Evaluate models using \gls{AUC}, calibration, and classification metrics across all designs.
  \item[-] Identify the most informative predictors of chronic disease onset and, through a predictor add-on analysis, assess whether domain specific features contribute independent predictive value beyond the core health burden set.
  \item[-] Document ethical, governance, and reproducibility practices for handling longitudinal pseudonymised health data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Contributions of the research}\label{sc:contributions}
This research makes contributions at three levels:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Empirical:} Demonstrates that longitudinal pooling substantially improves predictive performance and stability for chronic disease onset prediction in this cohort, and identifies the compact set of health burden indicators that drive this signal. Nutritional, psychosocial, and behavioural domain features added no independent predictive value beyond this core set, a finding discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:chapter5}.
  \item \textbf{Methodological:} Provides a reproducible pipeline for aligning longitudinal \gls{TILDA} data across domains and integrating features into \gls{ML} workflows. Full pipeline implementation, including the cleaning engine, scenario detection scripts, and modelling notebooks, is available at \url{https://gitlab.com/abderahman-msc-project/milestones/-/blob/main/README.md} (private repository, access granted on request).
  \item \textbf{Clinical:} Identifies the most informative predictors of chronic disease onset and evaluates the marginal contribution of domain specific features, providing a data driven basis for risk stratification.
\end{itemize}
